What happened to my Neighbors?

How the biggest decentralized memorial in the world reminds us of victims of the Nazis in many German cities and all over Europe.
What we can learn about them and our neighborhoods.

By Lisa-Marie Eckardt



Actually, each piece of the biggest decentralized memorial in the world is pretty small: Stumbling stones (in German: Stolpersteine) are tiny shiny stones in the pavement. But you can find them in the streets of almost every city in Germany, in many European countries and in Russia.

Each stone reminds us of a victim of the nazis, right in front of the house where they last lived. Each stone has an inscription: A name, the year the person was born and sometimes the year they died. But often you can only guess, when you read the word "deported".

Although the stones are called stumbling stones they are even in the ground and you will not stumble. So you have to take a closer look ... That is why I want to take you on a walk to find all of the stones in my neighborhood in Hamburg-Wilhelmsburg:



What I found out:

Compared to other parts of Hamburg there are just a few stumbling stones in my district called Wilhelmsburg.



The stones are memorials for people who were murdered, deported, distributed or driven to suizide by the Nazis.

In my neighbourhood some but not all of them where jewish ...



... others got deported for being homosexual ...



... or for being part of resistance groups.



What I found suprising:

Some of the victims got more than one stone in my district, plus a third one in another part of the city.

The project by the artist Gunter Demnig started in 1992. Today there are more than 100.000 stumbling stones. Maybe that sounds like a lot.

But 6 million jewish people where murdered in the Holocaust. And according to the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum (USHMM) there were about 17 million victims all together including a large number of sovjet, polish and serbian non-jewish civilians, prisioners of war, disabled people, Sinti and Roma, homosexual people and other groups. Political oponents and people who fought for the resistance are not includet in the USHMM statistic.

Hamburg has 6971 (July 2024) stumbling stones. You can find most of them in the districts Rotherbaum, Harvestehude and Eimsbttel which are more central, higher populated and wealthier parts of the city. But these districts also stand out, if you look at the stones per capita:

Today one stone costs 120 Euro. There are initiatives and organizations that collect donations as well as private sponsors. In 2023 the local sports club HSV donated a big number of stones in Rotherbaum. This district is where the University of Hamburg is located and it is also one of the richest.

But the district was also the center of Jewish life in Hamburg until the beginning of Nazi rule in 1933. In Rotherbaum was the main synagogue on Bornplatz, which was destroyed in 1938, and the Talmud Torah School.

That there are just a few stones in my neighborhood could have different reasons: The project is not complete yet, each stone needs a sponsor and my neighborhood is less wealthy than others. Also my district is on an island so it is a little remote and not as much populated (today but probabably also back then). Or was it maybe a place where people got more support to hide or leave the country? I have to do more research to answer that question.

In Berlin there are 10.755 stumbling stones. With more than 1600 stones the city district Charlottenburg has the most. There are streets with more than 100 victims 155 lived in Sybelstrae, 122 in Giesebrechtstrae. The district Wilmerdorf has more than 1400 and Mitte more than 1000.

164 victims with stumbling stones in Berlin had the surname Cohn. Among the 10 most common names are also Wolff (90), Hirsch (73), Lewin (69), Levy (67), Rosenthal (66), Meyer (64), Schlesinger (43), Blumenthal (41) and Goldstein (40).
